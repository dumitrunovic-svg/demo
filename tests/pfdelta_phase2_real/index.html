Abstract

Phase 2 transitions from the controlled nonlinear surrogate used in Phase 1 to a full real AC Power Flow (AC PF) computation using the pandapower library on the IEEE 118-bus network. This represents the first deployment of inZORi's Frozen Elite strategy on a real grid physics simulator rather than a mathematical approximation. The operational regime is intentionally challenging: base loads are scaled to 1.75√ó nominal (near voltage-collapse boundary), the Newton-Raphson iteration budget is constrained to nr_max=5 (tight convergence budget), and N-1 topology shocks (single-line outages) occur every 300 steps in Stage 3 ‚Äî a frequency roughly 3√ó higher than Phase 1's surrogate experiments. Under these conditions, static heuristic baselines (warm-start tracking and periodic reset) converge at approximately 52% in Stage 3. The inZORi Multi-Genome Frozen Elite selector ‚Äî an adaptive, O(1)-cost runtime mechanism choosing among six pre-evolved genomes based on contextual signals (season, recent convergence, N-1 status) ‚Äî achieves 69.6% S3 convergence, a +16.8 percentage points (pp) improvement over Baseline B. Recovery time after N-1 line restoration: inZORi recovers in 1.64 steps vs. 6.7 steps for baselines (~4√ó faster). All results are validated across 20 independent seeds with CI95 non-overlap confirmation.

2. Why Real AC PF is Harder

The transition from surrogate to real AC physics changes the difficulty landscape fundamentally:

  • Nonlinear coupling: In real AC PF, every bus voltage and phase angle affects every other through the full admittance matrix Y_bus. The surrogate's scalar nonlinearity is a simplification.
  • Near-collapse regime (1.75√ó): At 1.75√ó nominal load, the system operates close to the voltage-collapse boundary. Small perturbations in initial guess quality can cause NR divergence even with full iterations.
  • No continuation heuristics: We use plain NR with nr_max=5 and no continuation methods (no step-size control, no reactive power limiting heuristics) ‚Äî the solver either converges or fails.
  • N-1 topology changes (line outages): When a line is removed, the Y_bus matrix changes, invalidating warm-start guesses derived from the connected topology. Baselines have no mechanism to adapt; inZORi's genome encodes DC-init retry probability (jump_chance) which provides automatic fallback.

3. inZORi Multi-Genome Frozen Elite Strategy

3.1 What is the Frozen Elite Selector?

The Frozen Elite approach evolves a pool of diverse genomes offline (via ZOR evolutionary search, ~10 generations on the 118-bus task). At runtime, no evolution occurs ‚Äî the system selects from the pre-computed pool based on O(1) contextual heuristics:

  • Season-awareness: Aggressive genomes (high memory_lr, step_scale) preferred in S0/S1; conservative in S2/S3.
  • Convergence feedback: If recent window shows drops, switch to more stable genome.
  • N-1 detection: On topology shock, immediately switch to a genome with high jump_chance (DC-init retry probability), providing automatic flat-restart when the prior warm-start becomes invalid.

5.3 Baseline Definitions

  • Baseline A (warm-start tracking): Always uses the previous step's solution as initial guess. No fallback when topology changes.

7. Interpretation

7.1 Why Does Frozen Elite Work?

Under a 1.75√ó stressed regime with N-1 shocks every 300 steps in Stage 3, the warm-start approach of Baseline A and Baseline B fails for a systematic reason: topology changes invalidate prior solutions. A warm-start derived from a connected topology is not a good initial guess after a line disconnects. Without a fallback mechanism, NR diverges.

inZORi's genome encodes jump_chance ‚Äî a biologically-inspired "risk tolerance" that causes the selector to attempt a DC power flow warm-start (a simplified but topology-aware initial guess) when the standard warm-start fails. The N-1 recovery specialist genome (pool index 4, jump_chance=0.65) is automatically selected by the contextual selector upon detecting a topology shock, providing systematic recovery in under 2 steps on average.

7.2 Why ~52% Baseline Convergence?

The 52% Stage 3 convergence rate of both baselines reflects the genuine difficulty of the operating point:

  • 1.75√ó load means many buses operate near voltage limits.
  • N-1 events remove load paths, creating voltage violations on neighboring buses.
  • With nr_max=5, NR does not have enough iterations to converge from a degraded initial guess in these conditions.
  • The roughly equal performance of Baseline A and B in Stage 3 (despite very different strategies) indicates that in the N-1 shock regime, any fixed-strategy approach degrades similarly ‚Äî confirming that adaptive genome selection is the key differentiator.

7.3 Phase 1 vs Phase 2 Comparison

Phase 1 achieved 99.1% S3 convergence at nr_max=8 on a surrogate. Phase 2 achieves 69.6% at nr_max=5 on real physics. The absolute numbers are not directly comparable due to fundamentally different physics engines and difficulty regimes. What is consistent:

  • The advantage (pp) is stable: +15.2 pp (Phase 1) vs +16.8 pp (Phase 2).
  • Recovery time advantage is stable: ~7√ó (Phase 1) vs ~4√ó (Phase 2).
  • CI95 non-overlap holds in both phases.

This consistency across physics engines strengthens the claim that inZORi's genome-encoded adaptation mechanism is robust to the underlying simulation model.

8. What We Claim and Do Not Claim

8.1 What We Claim

  • A pre-evolved frozen genome pool, selected by O(1) contextual heuristics, achieves statistically significant (+16.8 pp, CI95 non-overlap) convergence improvement over static baselines in real AC PF under stressed conditions (1.75√ó load, N-1 every 300 steps).
  • Recovery time post-N-1 is ~4√ó faster due to the DC-init fallback mechanism encoded in genome parameters, requiring no online learning or computation beyond a genome lookup.
  • The advantage is validated across 20 seeds and holds consistently regardless of season, shock timing, or network state.

8.2 What We Do Not Claim

  • We do not claim that 69.6% convergence is "good" in absolute terms ‚Äî it reflects an extremely stressed regime. Real SCADA systems would not operate at 1.75√ó with this NR budget.
  • We do not claim optimality of the genome pool. The 6 genomes were derived from a 10-generation evolutionary search; additional evolution rounds would likely improve results.
  • We do not claim equivalence to commercial EMS/OPF solvers. This is a research comparison between evolutionary genome strategies and heuristic baselines.
  • We do not claim generalization to other network sizes or topologies without further experiments.

10. Conclusion

Phase 2 demonstrates that inZORi's Frozen Elite strategy ‚Äî offline-evolved genomes deployed at O(1) runtime cost ‚Äî maintains a statistically robust +16.8 percentage point convergence advantage and ~4√ó faster N-1 recovery over static heuristic baselines in real AC Power Flow on the IEEE 118-bus network under near-collapse load conditions. The mechanism is genome-encoded, not rule-programmed: the jump_chance parameter in the N-1 specialist genome provides automatic topology-adaptive behavior without explicit topology awareness in the selector logic.

Combined with Phase 1's 99.1% S3 convergence result on the surrogate model, this establishes a two-phase experimental foundation: (1) inZORi achieves near-perfect convergence when the physics regime permits it; (2) when the regime is at the physical limits (real AC, near-collapse, tight NR budget), inZORi's adaptive genome selection still delivers a consistent and significant advantage over static baselines.

The frozen elite approach has practical implications: genome evolution is done once, offline, on available compute. Deployment requires only a lookup table and a simple contextual selector ‚Äî no GPU, no neural network inference, no online gradient computation.
