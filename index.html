1. What is inZORi

inZORi is an engine for persistent artificial life that evolves behaviors instead of programming them. It starts from simple rules and expressive genomes; the system discovers complex adaptive strategies on its own, similar to natural evolution but accelerated.

  • Engine: populations of organisms with genomes that express differently depending on context (scene, task, environment).
  • Selection: fitness emerges from interactions (survival, resources, environment), not from predefined reward formulas.
  • Evolutionary memory (optional): organisms that learn from experience and pass on information via genomes or memory.

Result: emergent behavior – nobody coded the strategy; it arose from the pressure to stay viable.

2. How it works (brochure level)

  1. Task: a config file (JSON) describes the scenario: scenes (environment), population parameters, evaluator.
  2. Population: organisms with genomes (parameter vectors). Typical sizes: tens to thousands of individuals.
  3. Scene / environment: each organism lives in the same environment (grid, signal space, social world, etc.); the environment can be dynamic (phases, seasons, drift).
  4. Evaluator: a domain-specific module measures survival, resources, behavior – without being given an explicit reward function; fitness emerges from interactions.
  5. Selection and evolution: the most viable reproduce; the population evolves over generations. No forced convergence – diversity remains relevant.
  6. Result: adaptive strategies, reusable patterns, or ranking/prioritization, depending on the task – all discovered, not programmed.

Technical details: unified solver in zor_task_solver, evaluators per domain in problems/<name>/.

3. Current stage of inZORi

  • Status: system finished, production-ready for defined scenarios. Dozens of tests run across multiple domains.
  • Proven scale: 40→2000+ organisms, 600–800 steps per run, 20 seeds × 500 “years” (IQ), ~13k TESS signals, 100×100 grid, 5 phases (robotics), 3 seasons × cycles (traditions), static/slow/medium/chaotic drift (SDC).
  • What exists: unified solver (zor_task_solver), JSON tasks, domain evaluators (robotics, TESS, protein folding, Go, traditions, IQ/fertility, trading, drift continuity, etc.), HTML/PDF demo, real data for demo.
  • Documentation: this brochure is the canonical source; DEMO_*, QUICK_START, per-problem READMEs align with it. Export: PDF or website with clear sections and links to tests.

inZORi isn’t “better” in the sense of beating SOTA on a fixed benchmark – it’s different: for problems with no fixed solution, where environment and objectives change, and where you want emergent (not programmed) behavior.

5. Where inZORi is strong

  • Dynamic environments: hazards, resources, sensor noise that change over time – strategies that evolve without retraining.
  • Implicit objectives: survival, persistence, “staying viable” – without defining an explicit reward.
  • Versatility: the same engine across very different domains (navigation, folding, signal prioritization, social behavior).
  • Transparency and control: parameters, genomes, selection – you can see why certain patterns emerged.
  • Biological / social validation: patterns observed in nature or societies (e.g. fertility vs. education) can emerge from simple rules.
  • Realistic proxies: robots, astronomical signals, socio-economic populations – real-world applications without yet being production deployment.

6. Where inZORi is not the right fit

  • Tasks with a single correct answer (e.g. image classification with fixed labels) – RL/supervised are a better fit.
  • When you already have a well-trained model and only need fast inference – inZORi is for evolution/adaptation, not one-shot inference.
  • Strict low-latency requirements – evolution needs steps and populations; not for millisecond-level decisions.
  • “Who’s best” on classic benchmarks – inZORi isn’t optimized to beat AlphaGo or language models; it’s for scenarios where “best” is ill-defined or changes over time.

7. Sectors and use cases

  • Industrial / process optimization: processes that evolve toward efficiency under changing conditions.
  • Drug / molecule discovery: simulating molecule–protein interactions in parameter spaces.
  • Social / economic modeling: emergent behaviors in populations (fertility vs. IQ, traditions, resources).
  • Adaptive robotics: robots that learn strategies in unknown or changing environments (without retraining).
  • Risk and scenario analysis: evolutionary scenarios for strategic decisions under uncertainty.
  • Prioritization and exploration: ranking signals (e.g. TESS), exploring spaces without labels (clarity, stability, low noise).

8. Technology (brochure level, no proprietary detail)

  • Expressive genome: genes express differently depending on context (scene, environment).
  • Natural selection: fitness emerges from interactions, not predefined formulas.
  • Evolutionary memory: organisms learn from experience and can pass on information.
  • Scalability: from 40 to 2000+ organisms; parallelization on multi-CPU.
